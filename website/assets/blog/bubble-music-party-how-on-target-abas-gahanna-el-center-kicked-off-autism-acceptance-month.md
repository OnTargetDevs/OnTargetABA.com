This April, the Gahanna EL Center team made a commitment: every Tuesday of Autism Acceptance Month would feature a special sensory activity for the children in their care. Not because it was required, but because the children deserve to be celebrated, and celebration is most powerful when intentional.

## What Happened That Tuesday

The setup was deceptively simple: bubble machines, bubble guns, music, and the whole team. Children who struggle to engage became absorbed in tracking bubbles. Kids who find transitions difficult moved freely through the space. Children who rarely initiate physical interaction reached out instinctively toward bubbles, toward therapists, toward each other.

The music added rhythm and predictability that many autistic children find regulating. Some children hummed along. Some moved to the beat. Some became calmer in the presence of familiar, pleasant sound layered over the visual magic of bubbles.

The faces of children in that room told the whole story: open-mouthed wonder, full-body delight, the kind of happiness that simply exists because the moment is too good to contain.

Therapists weren't standing at a distance observing—they were in it, laughing, reaching for bubbles alongside children, celebrating every moment of connection and joy. This is what On Target ABA means when discussing showing up for their kids.

## Why Sensory Play Is So Much More Than Fun

The Bubble & Music Party was a celebration and therapy in action. Sensory play—activities that engage and stimulate sensory systems in intentional, enjoyable ways—is a core component of supporting autistic children at On Target ABA.

**Sensory regulation:** Many autistic children experience the world with heightened or altered sensory processing. Sounds are louder. Lights are brighter. Textures are more intense. Sensory play in a controlled, joyful context helps children practice tolerating and enjoying sensory input in a safe environment—building regulatory capacity that supports everything else.

**Visual tracking and attention:** Bubbles are extraordinary for this. They are unpredictable—they move, float, drift, and pop. Tracking them requires sustained visual attention across the full visual field. For children working on eye tracking and sustained focus, a room full of bubbles is a naturalistic, highly engaging exercise in exactly those skills.

**Reaching and gross motor development:** The instinct to reach for a bubble—to catch it, touch it, or pop it—activates gross and fine motor pathways in a way that feels effortless and playful. Children who might resist structured gross motor exercises participate enthusiastically when the goal is catching a bubble.

**Social initiation and joint attention:** Sensory play in a group context creates natural opportunities for joint attention—shared focus on a common object or event that is a foundational building block of social connection. When two children both reach for the same bubble, they practice joint attention without clinical instruction.

**Communication opportunities:** Bubbles create reasons to communicate. "More bubbles!" is often one of the first functional requests children with limited communication make in sensory play contexts. "Look at that one!" is an initiation. Reaching toward a therapist to direct their attention is a gesture. The activity creates a naturally communication-rich environment.

**Regulation through music:** Music engages the brain differently from other stimuli. The predictable rhythm provides scaffolding that many autistic children find organizing—it gives structure to a moment, predictability to a space, and a sensory anchor that helps regulate arousal levels. Music therapy as a standalone intervention has a robust evidence base, and integration into sensory play extends those benefits into everyday contexts.

Every bubble that floated through that room was doing clinical work—because quality ABA therapy and genuine joy live in the same space.

April is full of language about autism awareness and acceptance. The shift from "awareness" to "acceptance" represents meaningful evolution in how the world thinks about autistic people. But language is only the beginning—what matters is what acceptance looks like in practice.

Autism acceptance looks like a team of therapists who choose to spend April Tuesdays planning sensory celebrations for children in their care—not because anyone asked them to, but because they wanted to.

It looks like a room that says to every child: you are celebrated here, exactly as you are. Not a version of you that is more typical, more quiet, more still. You—the child who reaches for bubbles with their whole body, who makes sounds of pure delight, who stims with joy when music plays.

It looks like professionals who understand that a child's laugh is data—that the quality of a child's experience in therapy matters enormously, not just as a means to clinical ends but as a value in itself.

It looks like a Bubble & Music Party.

At On Target ABA, acceptance is not a month. It is a daily practice, visible in small decisions that people in their centers make every day. The team at the Gahanna EL Center embodies that practice.

## Why Sensory Activities Work So Well in ABA Therapy Settings

The research on sensory play in early childhood is extensive, and its integration into ABA therapy settings reflects growing understanding that the most effective therapy is the therapy children actively want to participate in.

**Motivation:** ABA therapy relies on motivation—the use of reinforcing activities and items to make learning worth the effort. When therapy itself is genuinely enjoyable, intrinsic motivation rises. Children having fun are more engaged, more communicative, and more willing to try new things.

**Naturalistic learning:** The Natural Environment Teaching approach—a core component of quality ABA—emphasizes embedding learning opportunities into naturally occurring, enjoyable activities. Sensory play creates exactly this: a rich, naturalistic context full of opportunities to practice communication, social, motor, and cognitive skills without artificiality of table-based drill.

**Generalization:** Skills learned in highly enjoyable contexts tend to generalize more readily than skills learned in neutral or aversive ones. When a child learns to request, initiate, or make eye contact in the context of something they love, that skill is more likely to appear in other enjoyable contexts across their day.

**Therapeutic relationship:** The relationship between a child and their RBT is the foundation of everything else in ABA therapy. Shared positive experiences—like a Bubble & Music Party—deepen that relationship in ways that improve the effectiveness of all subsequent therapy. A child who danced through bubbles with their therapist trusts that therapist more fully.
