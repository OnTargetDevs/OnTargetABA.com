# What Is Extended School Year (ESY)? A Complete Guide for Families of Children with Autism

### AI Summary

Extended School Year (ESY) services provide eligible students with disabilities — including children with autism — continued educational support beyond the traditional 180-day school year. This guide breaks down what ESY is, how eligibility is determined, what services are included, and how ABA therapy through providers like On Target ABA complements ESY to help children maintain and build critical skills year-round.

## Summer Break Can Mean More Than Rest — It Can Mean Regression

For most families, the end of the school year brings a collective exhale. But for parents of children with autism, summer often comes with different concerns — worry that skills their child worked hard to build during school may slip away before fall arrives.

This concern is well-founded. Many clinicians and families observe that children with Autism Spectrum Disorder are particularly vulnerable to skill regression during extended breaks from structured learning environments. Routines disappear. Therapies pause. The predictable rhythms that help autistic children feel safe are suddenly replaced with unstructured days.

Extended School Year services exist because of this reality. They represent one of the most powerful, yet frequently underutilized, tools available to families navigating special education.

## What Is Extended School Year (ESY)?

Extended School Year describes educational and related services provided to eligible students with disabilities beyond the traditional 180-day school calendar. Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), school districts must make ESY services available to students with disabilities when deemed necessary to provide a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE).

ESY is not the same as summer school. Traditional summer school is a general program for students needing academic catch-up. ESY is entirely individualized — designed around a specific student's IEP goals, documented areas of vulnerability, and particular skills at risk of regression.

Because ESY is a component of FAPE, it is provided at no cost to families. The school district funds and delivers services outlined in the student's IEP.

ESY services can include:

- Continued speech-language therapy
- Occupational therapy
- Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) services
- Behavior management support
- Social skills instruction
- Academic instruction targeting IEP goals
- Life skills and daily living skill development

The intensity, frequency, duration, and location of ESY services are determined by the student's IEP team — not by a general school-wide program.

## The Legal Foundation: What IDEA Requires

The right to ESY services is grounded in federal law. Under IDEA, every public school district must ensure ESY services are available to students with disabilities when the IEP team determines that those services are necessary for FAPE provision.

Important points for families:

**ESY must be considered annually for every student with a disability.** Having an IEP automatically triggers an obligation for the school district to evaluate whether ESY is needed each year. However, having an IEP does not automatically make a child eligible — eligibility must be determined based on specific circumstances and needs.

**Parents are full members of the IEP team.** As with all IEP decisions, parents have a seat at the table when ESY eligibility is discussed. Your observations and documentation of your child's behavior during previous breaks are valid contributions.

**Denial can be appealed.** If the school district determines your child is not eligible for ESY and you disagree, you have the right to appeal that decision.

## How Is ESY Eligibility Determined?

ESY eligibility is not based on a single factor or simple checklist. The IEP team evaluates each student individually, with the central question: will educational benefits gained during the regular school year be significantly jeopardized if services are not continued during school breaks?

The two primary factors courts and IEP teams use are:

### 1. Regression and Recoupment

Regression refers to loss of previously acquired skills. Recoupment refers to how long it takes to relearn those skills after a break in services.

For children with autism, regression during unstructured breaks is well-documented. A student who learned to communicate a basic need, tolerate transitions, or follow instructions may lose significant ground during summer without support — and require weeks or months recovering that progress. When recoupment time significantly exceeds expectations for typical students, ESY is often warranted.

### 2. Critical Stage of Development

This factor recognizes that some students are at pivotal developmental points where service disruption could have outsized consequences. For example, a child on the verge of a communication breakthrough, developing early literacy skills requiring consistent reinforcement, or working toward functional life skills affecting independence may qualify under this criterion even without documented significant regression.

Additional factors IEP teams may consider:

- The degree to which the student's disability affects their ability to maintain skills
- The nature and severity of the student's disability
- Whether the student is in a critical learning period
- Whether specific IEP goals involve skills necessary for self-sufficiency or independence
- The student's rate of progress and sensitivity to interruption

In practice, only a minority of students receiving special education services are ultimately determined eligible for ESY — eligibility decisions are made case-by-case, not by formula. This makes it important for parents to come prepared to IEP meetings with specific observations, data, and documentation.

## ESY vs. Summer School: Understanding the Difference

Understanding this distinction is important, because confusion between the two can lead parents to accept summer school placement when their child actually needs and is entitled to ESY.

| Aspect | Extended School Year (ESY) | Summer School |
|--------|---------------------------|---------------|
| Who it's for | Students with IEPs who meet ESY eligibility criteria | General student population |
| Cost to family | Free (part of FAPE) | May have a cost |
| Content | Individualized to IEP goals | General academic curriculum |
| Purpose | Maintain skills and prevent regression | Academic remediation or enrichment |
| Legal basis | Required under IDEA when necessary | Optional school program |
| Related services | May include OT, speech, ABA, behavior support | Typically not included |

If your child's school district offers a summer program and suggests it fulfills the ESY requirement, ask specifically how that program addresses your child's individualized IEP goals. A general summer program does not automatically satisfy a school district's ESY obligation.

## Signs Your Child May Benefit From ESY

Not every parent of an autistic child will have had a formal conversation about ESY. If unsure whether your child might benefit, consider these signs:

- Your child shows noticeable skill loss after weekends, holidays, or winter and spring breaks
- It takes your child significantly longer than expected to "get back on track" after a break
- Your child's IEP team has noted that progress is fragile or highly dependent on consistent support
- Your child is working toward a skill requiring daily repetition and structured reinforcement to maintain
- Your child struggles significantly with transitions back to the school environment after extended time away
- Your child's therapists (speech, OT, ABA) have noted regression during previous breaks

If any of these patterns sound familiar, request a conversation with your child's IEP team specifically about ESY. Come to that meeting with whatever documentation you have — your own notes, therapist observations, communication logs — to paint as clear a picture as possible of what breaks look like for your child.

## How ABA Therapy Supports and Complements ESY

ABA therapy and ESY services are not mutually exclusive — in fact, they are most powerful when working together. For many children with autism, ESY services provide the school-based structure and goal maintenance during breaks, while ABA therapy through a provider like On Target ABA offers intensive, individualized behavioral support continuing regardless of the school calendar.

How ABA therapy specifically supports ESY goals:

**Skill maintenance through consistent reinforcement.** ABA therapists use evidence-based strategies to reinforce skills a child is working on in school, reducing regression likelihood during breaks. When ABA providers and school teams are aligned on goals and strategies, children experience seamless consistency across environments.

**Data-driven progress tracking.** ABA therapy generates detailed behavioral data serving as powerful documentation for ESY eligibility discussions. If your ABA provider has tracked your child's performance before and after previous school breaks, that data tells a concrete regression and recoupment story that strengthens your case at IEP meetings.

**Generalization of skills across settings.** One core goal of ABA is helping children apply skills learned in one environment across multiple settings. ESY keeps school-based skills alive during summer; ABA therapy ensures those skills generalize to the home, community, and therapeutic setting.

**Behavior support and emotional regulation.** Extended breaks can increase anxiety, sensory dysregulation, and behavioral challenges for autistic children thriving on routine. ABA therapy provides ongoing behavioral support helping children stay regulated and engaged even when school structure is absent.

## How to Advocate for ESY for Your Child

If you believe your child may benefit from ESY services, here is a practical roadmap for advocating effectively:

1. **Review your child's current IEP** and identify the goals that are most fragile or most dependent on consistent reinforcement. These are your starting point.

2. **Document your observations** from previous school breaks. Write down specific examples of skills that declined, behaviors that increased, or how long it took to recover progress. The more specific and concrete, the better.

3. **Communicate with your child's service providers.** Ask your child's ABA therapist, speech pathologist, and occupational therapist if they have observed regression during previous breaks and whether they would support an ESY recommendation in writing.

4. **Request an IEP meeting** to specifically discuss ESY eligibility before the end of the school year. ESY decisions should ideally be made during the annual IEP meeting, but you can request a meeting at any time.

5. **Know your rights.** If ESY is denied and you disagree, you can request mediation, file a state complaint, or request a due process hearing. Organizations like Autism Speaks, Wrightslaw, and your state's Parent Training and Information Center (PTI) can provide guidance and support.

6. **Partner with On Target ABA.** Our team can help you gather documentation, align therapeutic goals with school IEP goals, and provide the kind of consistent year-round support that gives your child the best possible foundation — whether ESY is approved or not.
